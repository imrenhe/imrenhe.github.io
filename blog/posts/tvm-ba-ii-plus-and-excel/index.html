Time Value of Money on the BA II Plus and in Excel

A side-by-side undergraduate guide to solving TVM problems with the TI BA II Plus calculator and Microsoft Excel — from setup to mortgages, NPV, and IRR.

Why Two Tools?

Most introductory finance problems can be cracked two ways: on a TI BA II Plus financial calculator or in a spreadsheet. The calculator is what you bring to an exam; Excel is what you use at work. They give the same answer, but the workflow is different — the calculator stores one problem in memory, while Excel makes inputs visible and easy to tweak.

This post is a practical walkthrough for an undergraduate finance student. We start with calculator setup, move through the core TVM problems, and finish with NPV and IRR for uneven cash flows. Every example shows both the calculator keystrokes and the Excel formula.

Setting Up the BA II Plus

Out of the box, the BA II Plus has two defaults that trip up students. Fix them once.

1. Set decimal places to 4 (or more). Press 2nd → FORMAT → enter 4 → ENTER → 2nd → QUIT.

2. Set payments per year to 1. Press 2nd → P/Y → enter 1 → ENTER → 2nd → QUIT. This is the safer default. When you have monthly compounding, you can either change P/Y to 12 or leave P/Y at 1 and enter the periodic rate manually (e.g., 0.5 for 6% / 12). I recommend the second approach — fewer hidden settings to forget.

3. Confirm END mode (not BGN). Most problems use end-of-period cash flows. Press 2nd → BGN. If it says END, press 2nd → QUIT. If it says BGN, press 2nd → SET → 2nd → QUIT.

4. Clear the TVM registers before every new problem. Press 2nd → CLR TVM. The calculator remembers your last problem; failing to clear is the #1 source of wrong answers on exams.

TipSign convention

The BA II Plus uses cash flow signs. Money you pay out is negative; money you receive is positive. If PV and FV have the same sign, the calculator returns an error. This convention matches Excel.

The Five TVM Keys

Every basic time value problem uses some subset of five variables, four of which you know and one you solve for.

Key Meaning Excel argument
N Number of periods nper
I/Y Interest rate per period (as a percent) rate
PV Present value (lump sum today) pv
PMT Recurring payment each period pmt
FV Future value (lump sum at the end) fv

One pitfall: in the BA II Plus, you enter I/Y as a percent (5 means 5%), but Excel’s rate argument wants a decimal (0.05). Forgetting this is the #2 source of wrong answers.

The next four examples walk through solving for each of the lump-sum unknowns — FV, PV, N, and I/Y — one at a time.

Example 1 — Solving for FV (future value of a lump sum)

You deposit $1,000 today in an account earning 5% per year. What is the balance in 10 years?

BA II Plus:

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd CLR TVM 0
2 10 N N = 10
3 5 I/Y I/Y = 5
4 1000 +/− PV PV = −1,000
5 0 PMT PMT = 0
6 CPT FV FV = 1,628.89

Excel: =FV(0.05, 10, 0, -1000) → 1,628.89

Example 2 — Solving for PV (present value of a lump sum)

You’d like to have $50,000 saved for a down payment in 5 years. Your savings earn 6% per year. How much do you need to deposit today?

BA II Plus:

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd CLR TVM 0
2 5 N N = 5
3 6 I/Y I/Y = 6
4 0 PMT PMT = 0
5 50000 FV FV = 50,000
6 CPT PV PV = −37,362.91

The negative sign means it’s an outflow today — you pay $37,362.91 now to receive $50,000 in five years.

Excel: =PV(0.06, 5, 0, 50000) → −37,362.91

Example 3 — Solving for N (how long?)

You invest $5,000 today at 8% per year. How many years until it doubles to $10,000?

BA II Plus:

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd CLR TVM 0
2 8 I/Y I/Y = 8
3 5000 +/− PV PV = −5,000
4 0 PMT PMT = 0
5 10000 FV FV = 10,000
6 CPT N N = 9.0065

So it takes a little over nine years. (This is a quick check on the “Rule of 72”: 72 / 8 = 9 years, which is close.)

Excel: =NPER(0.08, 0, -5000, 10000) → 9.0065

Example 4 — Solving for I/Y (what rate did I earn?)

A friend tells you they bought a stock for $1,000 six years ago and just sold it for $1,500. What annualized rate of return did they earn?

BA II Plus:

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd CLR TVM 0
2 6 N N = 6
3 1000 +/− PV PV = −1,000
4 0 PMT PMT = 0
5 1500 FV FV = 1,500
6 CPT I/Y I/Y = 6.9913

Roughly 6.99% per year.

Excel: =RATE(6, 0, -1000, 1500) → 0.069913

Example 5 — Future value of an annuity (monthly contributions)

Now add PMT to the mix.

You save $300 at the end of every month for 30 years in an account earning 7% APR compounded monthly. What’s the balance at the end?

The trick: convert annual quantities to monthly before you enter them. Periodic rate = 7% / 12 ≈ 0.5833%. Number of periods = 30 × 12 = 360.

BA II Plus:

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd CLR TVM 0
2 360 N N = 360
3 7 ÷ 12 = I/Y I/Y = 0.5833
4 0 PV PV = 0
5 300 +/− PMT PMT = −300
6 CPT FV FV = 365,990.62

Excel: =FV(0.07/12, 360, -300, 0) → 365,990.62

APR vs EAR

The annual percentage rate (APR) is the stated, nominal rate. The effective annual rate (EAR) is what you actually earn or pay once compounding is accounted for. For any compounding frequency above 1, EAR > APR.

Example 6 — Converting APR to EAR

A credit card charges 18% APR compounded monthly. What’s the EAR?

BA II Plus: the ICONV worksheet does this in three keystrokes.

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd ICONV NOM =
2 18 ENTER ↓ C/Y =
3 12 ENTER ↑ CPT EFF = 19.5618

Excel: =EFFECT(0.18, 12) → 0.195618

Going the other way — from EAR to APR — use NOMINAL: =NOMINAL(0.195618, 12) → 0.18

Mortgages

A mortgage is just a present-value annuity problem in disguise. The bank lends you the present value; you repay with level monthly payments that include both principal and interest.

Example 7 — Monthly payment

You borrow $400,000 on a 30-year mortgage at 6.5% APR (compounded monthly). What’s the monthly payment?

BA II Plus:

Step Keystrokes Display
1 2nd CLR TVM 0
2 360 N N = 360
3 6.5 ÷ 12 = I/Y I/Y = 0.5417
4 400000 PV PV = 400,000
5 0 FV FV = 0
6 CPT PMT PMT = −2,528.27

The negative sign means you’re paying out each month.

Excel: =PMT(0.065/12, 360, 400000) → −2,528.27

Each monthly payment is split between interest (charged on the outstanding balance) and principal (which reduces that balance). Early on, the balance is huge, so almost all of the payment is interest. Late in the loan, the balance is small, and most of the payment goes to principal. Excel exposes this split directly: IPMT returns the interest portion of a given payment and PPMT returns the principal portion. For month 1 of this loan:

  • =IPMT(0.065/12, 1, 360, 400000) → −2,166.67
  • =PPMT(0.065/12, 1, 360, 400000) → −361.60

Together they sum to the full payment. By month 360, the proportions are reversed.

Uneven Cash Flows: NPV

When the cash flows aren’t level, the basic TVM keys aren’t enough — you discount each cash flow on its own and add them up. On the BA II Plus, this is the CF (cash flow) worksheet.

Example 8 — NPV of a project

A project costs $10,000 today. It pays $2,000 at the end of year 1, $3,000 in year 2, $4,000 in year 3, and $5,000 in year 4. The discount rate is 10%. What’s the NPV?

BA II Plus: the CF worksheet stores cash flows by period.

Step Keystrokes Display
1 CF 2nd CLR WORK CF0 = 0
2 10000 +/− ENTER ↓ CF0 = −10,000
3 2000 ENTER ↓ ↓ C01 = 2,000
4 3000 ENTER ↓ ↓ C02 = 3,000
5 4000 ENTER ↓ ↓ C03 = 4,000
6 5000 ENTER ↓ ↓ C04 = 5,000
7 NPV 10 ENTER ↓ CPT NPV = 717.85

The ↓ ↓ between cash flows skips past the “frequency” prompt (F01, F02, …). If you have the same cash flow repeated several years in a row, you can enter the value once and set the frequency instead of typing it multiple times.

Excel: Excel’s NPV function discounts the first cash flow back one period, so you have to add the time-zero outflow separately.

=NPV(0.10, 2000, 3000, 4000, 5000) - 10000 → 717.85

WarningThe =NPV gotcha

Excel’s NPV assumes the first argument is one period away. The time-zero cash flow goes outside the function. Many wrong answers come from including -10000 inside the NPV parentheses.

IRR

The internal rate of return is the discount rate that makes NPV exactly zero. It’s the project’s break-even rate.

Example 9 — IRR of the same project

BA II Plus: with the cash flows from Example 8 still loaded, just press:

IRR CPT → IRR = 12.8258

Excel: put the cash flows in a column (including the −10,000 at time zero) and call:

=IRR(A1:A5) → 0.128258

Because the IRR (12.83%) is greater than the 10% discount rate used in Example 8, the project’s NPV is positive — exactly what we found. In general, accept a project when IRR > the required rate of return.

NoteWhen IRR misleads

IRR can have multiple solutions when cash flows change sign more than once (e.g., −, +, +, −). For mutually exclusive projects, IRR can also rank projects differently than NPV. NPV is the safer decision rule; IRR is the more intuitive one to communicate.

Cheat Sheet

Task BA II Plus Excel
Future value of lump sum N I/Y PV 0 PMT CPT FV =FV(rate, nper, 0, -pv)
Present value of lump sum N I/Y FV 0 PMT CPT PV =PV(rate, nper, 0, -fv)
Number of periods I/Y PV PMT FV CPT N =NPER(rate, pmt, pv, fv)
Periodic rate N PV PMT FV CPT I/Y =RATE(nper, pmt, pv, fv)
Annuity payment N I/Y PV FV CPT PMT =PMT(rate, nper, pv, fv)
APR → EAR 2nd ICONV, enter NOM and C/Y, CPT EFF =EFFECT(apr, m)
EAR → APR 2nd ICONV, enter EFF and C/Y, CPT NOM =NOMINAL(ear, m)
NPV (uneven cash flows) CF worksheet, then NPV rate ENTER ↓ CPT =NPV(rate, cf1:cfn) + cf0
IRR CF worksheet, then IRR CPT =IRR(cf0:cfn)
Interest portion of payment (Calculate manually from balance × rate) =IPMT(rate, period, nper, pv)
Principal portion of payment (Payment minus interest portion) =PPMT(rate, period, nper, pv)

Closing Thoughts

Three habits will save you in this class:

  1. Always clear the calculator (2nd CLR TVM for TVM keys, 2nd CLR WORK inside CF) before starting a new problem.
  2. Match the period. If the problem is monthly, your N, I/Y, and PMT all need to be monthly. Mixing annual and monthly is the most common error.
  3. Watch the signs. If you put money in (a loan, an investment), it’s negative. If you take money out (a withdrawal, a repayment), it’s positive. Excel and the BA II Plus follow the same rule.

When you graduate, you’ll do almost everything in Excel — once a problem is bigger than the five TVM keys, the spreadsheet wins. But knowing both makes you faster, and it gives you a sanity check: if the calculator and the spreadsheet disagree, you’ve made a setup mistake somewhere, and you’ll catch it before turning it in.
